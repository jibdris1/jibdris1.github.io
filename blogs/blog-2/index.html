AI’s Power Outpaces Its Principles

Jibril Idris | Jul 7, 2026 min read

Introduction

Artificial intelligence has become a defining force in the modern infosphere — a term Luciano Floridi (2023) uses to describe the integrated environment where humans, digital systems, and information processes interact. AI systems now influence hiring decisions, medical diagnoses, credit scoring, policing, and even the content individuals encounter online. With this influence comes profound ethical responsibility. Yet as Floridi argues, AI is not merely a tool; it is an “agent-like technology” capable of shaping human behavior and social structures. This raises a critical question: How do we ensure AI systems behave ethically when regulation alone cannot keep pace with technological change? This blog explores that question through the lens of AI governance, soft ethics, and data science ethics, using Amazon’s failed hiring algorithm as a real-world case study. Drawing on Floridi’s framework, it argues that responsible AI requires more than compliance — it requires a proactive ethical culture.

Case Study: Amazon’s Biased Hiring Algorithm

In 2018, Amazon discontinued an internal AI hiring tool after discovering it systematically downgraded résumés from women. The model had been trained on historical hiring data from a male-dominated tech workforce. As a result, it learned to penalize terms like “women’s chess club” and favor language more commonly used by men.

This case illustrates several of Floridi’s key ethical concerns:

• Algorithmic bias — The system reproduced and amplified historical discrimination.

• Opacity — Developers struggled to understand why the model behaved as it did, reflecting Floridi’s concern about “black-box epistemology.”

• Lack of accountability — No governance structure existed to evaluate the model before deployment.

• Data ethics failures — The training data violated principles of fairness and representativeness.

• Governance gaps — No regulatory framework prevented the deployment of such a system. Amazon’s hiring algorithm is not an isolated failure — it is a symptom of deeper structural issues in AI governance.

Ethical Foundations: Why Regulation Is Not Enough

Floridi argues that AI ethics must be understood through two complementary lenses:

i. Hard Ethics (Regulation, Law, Compliance) These are formal, enforceable rules — such as the EU AI Act or Canada’s AIDA — designed to prevent harm and ensure accountability.

ii. Soft Ethics (Voluntary, Proactive, Organizational Practices) These are internal norms, guidelines, and cultural commitments that go beyond legal requirements.

This emphasizes that hard ethics alone cannot govern AI for three reasons:

i. AI evolves faster than legislation By the time laws are drafted, debated, and enacted, the technology has already changed.

ii.Regulation is necessarily general Laws cannot specify how to design a fair model, how to audit training data, or how to interpret ambiguous ethical trade-offs.

iii. AI operates in diverse contexts No single regulatory framework can anticipate every domain-specific risk.

This is why for a “distributed governance ecosystem” soft ethics plays a central and an important role.

Soft Ethics: The Ethical Layer AI Cannot Function Without

Soft ethics refers to voluntary, proactive ethical practices that organizations adopt to guide AI development. According to Floridi, soft ethics is essential because it:

• Encourages responsible innovation

• Fosters ethical reflection

• Addresses context-specific risks

• Builds trustworthiness

• Compensates for regulatory gaps

Soft ethics includes:

• Ethical guidelines

• Internal review boards

• Model documentation

• Impact assessments

• Stakeholder engagement

• Organizational culture-building

Soft ethics should not only be optional but should be the anchor or foundation of the “moral infrastructure” that enables AI to support human flourishing.

Applying Data Science Ethics to AI Systems

The ethical principles of AI applications including fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, and data stewardship are foundational to AI ethics. Floridi emphasizes that AI inherits all the ethical challenges of data science and introduces new ones.

Application of these principles to Amazon’s hiring algorithm:

i. Fairness

The model discriminated against women because it learned from biased data. A fairness audit could have detected this early.

ii. Transparency

The system lacked explainability. Floridi calls this the “opacity problem,” which undermines accountability and trust.

iii. Accountability

No clear governance structure existed to approve or monitor the model. Floridi stresses that accountability must be “distributed but traceable.”

iv. Data Quality

Historical hiring data encoded systemic bias. Ethical AI requires evaluating whether data is fit for moral purpose, not just fit for technical purpose.

v. Privacy

Although not central in this case, hiring data often contains sensitive information. Floridi argues for “privacy as contextual integrity,” meaning data must be used appropriately within its social context.

Building Ethical AI

Drawing on the principles stated above, here is a practical governance model for responsible AI:

a. Ethical Design Principles

Before development begins, teams should define:

• What values the system must uphold

• What harms must be avoided

• What trade-offs are acceptable

This aligns with Floridi’s call for “ethics by design.”

b. Data Governance and Quality Controls

Ethical AI requires:

• Bias testing

• Representative datasets

• Documentation of data provenance

• Clear consent and privacy protections

Floridi emphasizes that data must be evaluated not only for accuracy but for ethical adequacy.

c. Algorithmic Transparency

Explainability tools help stakeholders understand:

• Why the model made a decision

• What features influenced the outcome

• Whether the model behaves consistently

Transparency is essential for what Floridi calls “epistemic responsibility.”

d. Accountability Structures

Organizations should establish:

• AI ethics committees

• Model approval workflows

• Post-deployment monitoring

• Clear ownership of decisions

Floridi argues that accountability must be “shared but not diluted.”

e. Soft Ethics Integration

Soft ethics encourages:

• Ethical reflection

• Stakeholder engagement

• Scenario analysis

• Continuous learning

This is where culture meets governance.

Why This Matters: AI Shapes Human Futures

AI systems increasingly determine:

• Who gets hired

• Who receives medical care

• Who gets approved for loans

• Who is flagged by police

• What information people see

Without ethical safeguards, AI can reinforce inequality, erode trust, and cause real harm. Floridi warns that unethical AI risks damaging not only individuals but the entire infosphere.

Conclusion: Ethical AI Requires More Than Good Intentions

Floridi’s central message is clear: AI ethics is not just about preventing harm — it is about enabling human flourishing. Regulation provides the outer boundaries, but soft ethics provides the inner compass.

To build trustworthy AI, organizations must combine:

• Strong governance

• Ethical design

• Transparent algorithms

• Responsible data practices

• A culture of accountability

The Amazon hiring algorithm failed because it lacked these safeguards. But its failure teaches us a crucial lesson: ethical AI is not a technical challenge — it is a moral one. And it begins long before the algorithm is deployed.
